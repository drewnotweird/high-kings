# How to Play — Content & Illustration Brief

---

## INTRO

**Copy:**
Before chess, before checkers, there was Hnefatafl.

The Vikings played this game. So did the Celts, the Anglo-Saxons, and the peoples of the far north. Boards have been found in Viking graves, in Irish monasteries, scratched into stone in Orkney. For nearly a thousand years — roughly 400 AD to 1400 AD — it was the strategy game of northern Europe.

Then chess arrived from the south, and Hnefatafl was forgotten. Almost.

The rules survived in fragments: a mention in a Norse saga here, a manuscript illustration there, a living tradition among the Saami people of Scandinavia that never quite died out. Scholars pieced them back together. And now you're playing it.

Each icon fades in one at a time, as if being discovered. The map has no political borders, just coastlines drawn in thin lines. Calm, atmospheric, slow.

## PART ONE: THE CORE GAME

These rules apply to every variant.

---

### 1. The Board

**Copy:**
Hnefatafl is played on a square grid. The centre square is the **Throne** — where the King begins. The four corner squares are **escape squares** — ancient sanctuaries the King must reach to win.

**Illustration: `board-overview`**
Top-down 2D line drawing of a simplified grid (7×7 is enough to read clearly). Throne marked at centre with a rune symbol. Four corners each marked with a distinct symbol. Slow animated pulse breathes on the corners and throne simultaneously — a gentle glow, looping.

---

### 2. Two Sides, One Goal Each

**Copy:**
This is an asymmetric game — the two sides play nothing alike.

The **Defenders** protect a King. Their goal is to escort him to a corner escape square. They are outnumbered.

The **Attackers** surround the board. Their goal is to capture the King before he reaches safety. They have the numbers.

**Illustration: `two-sides`**
Split view. Left side: a small tight cluster of lighter pieces around a taller King. Right side: a larger spread of darker pieces. Both sides animate with a slow breathe — in, out, in, out. No movement, just presence.

---

### 3. How Pieces Move

**Copy:**
Every piece — attacker, defender, and King alike — moves the same way: any number of squares in a straight line, horizontally or vertically. No diagonals. No jumping over other pieces.

The corners and the Throne are off-limits to all pieces except the King.

**Illustration: `movement`**
A single piece on a small grid section. Animated dotted lines extend outward from it in all four cardinal directions. Lines stop at the board edge on three sides and at a blocking piece on the fourth. The lines animate in sequence — top, right, bottom, left — then fade and repeat.

---

### 4. Capturing

**Copy:**
Capture an enemy piece by sandwiching it between two of your own on a straight line — one on each side. The trapped piece is removed immediately.

You have to move to complete the trap. A piece sitting between two enemies is perfectly safe until you close the other side.

**Illustration: `capture`**
Three pieces in a horizontal row: attacker — defender — attacker. The right attacker is missing. It slides in from off-screen to complete the sandwich. The middle defender ripples and fades. Scene resets and loops.

---

### 5. The Throne & Corners Assist Captures

**Copy:**
Empty special squares — the Throne and all four corners — count as phantom captors. You only need one friendly piece to complete a capture if an empty special square covers the other side.

**Illustration: `hostile-squares`**
A corner square at the edge of the board. A defender piece sits one square away from it. A single attacker slides in from the opposite side. The defender is caught between the attacker and the corner — and disappears. Loops.

---

### 6. Capturing the King

**Copy:**
The King cannot be captured like a normal piece. He must be surrounded on **all four sides** simultaneously — by attackers or by empty special squares. As long as even one side is open, he is safe.

**Illustration: `king-capture`**
The King on the Throne. Three attackers already in place on three sides. The fourth attacker slides in to seal the last gap. The King flashes briefly and fades. Loops.

---

### 7. The King Escapes

**Copy:**
Move the King to any corner square and the Defenders win. The Attackers must keep every path to every corner blocked — all game long.

**Illustration: `king-escape`**
The King near the centre of the board. A clear horizontal path leads to a corner. The King slides smoothly along it and arrives at the corner, which briefly flares with light. Loops.

## PART TWO: THE VARIANTS

Each variant shares the core rules above. Below is only what's different.

---

### Copenhagen (11×11)

**The standard game. Use this if you're not sure where to start.**

**What's different: Shieldwall captures**

A line of two or more enemy pieces pressed against the board edge can be wiped out in a single move — if you flank both ends simultaneously (a corner counts as one flank). The King cannot be taken this way.

**Illustration: `shieldwall`**
Three defender pieces in a row against the top edge. An attacker already covers one outer flank. A second attacker slides in to cover the other. All three defenders vanish together. Loops.

---

### Tawlbwrdd (11×11)

**Same board and pieces as Copenhagen, but the King has more freedom.**

**What's different: Edge escape**

The King wins by reaching **any square on the board edge** — not just the four corners. Much harder to contain him; the Attackers must hold the entire perimeter.

Also includes shieldwall captures.

**Illustration: `edge-escape`**
The King near the edge. A dotted highlight runs along the entire board edge — the full perimeter glows faintly. The King slides to an edge square (not a corner). Victory flash. Loops.

---

### Linnaeus Tablut (9×9)

**A smaller, faster game recorded by the botanist Carl Linnaeus in 1732.**

**What's different: Weak King + edge escape**

Once the King steps off the Throne, he can be sandwiched like any other piece — just two attackers, one on each side, is enough. He still escapes to any edge square, not just corners. Play is more urgent and aggressive.

**Illustration: `weak-king`**
The King off the Throne, caught between two attackers in a horizontal sandwich. No special four-sided surround needed — just the two. He fades. Loops.

---

### Saami Tablut (9×9)

**The living tradition — still played today by the Saami people of northern Scandinavia.**

**What's different: Wider defender starting formation**

Same rules as Linnaeus Tablut (weak King, edge escape), but the defenders begin in a broader diamond pattern. A slightly more open and tactical opening.

**Illustration: `saami-start`**
Side-by-side starting positions: Linnaeus defenders in a tight cross, Saami defenders in a wider spread. Both animated with a gentle pulse to draw the eye to the difference.

---

### Brandub (7×7)

**The Irish variant. Small board, few pieces, pure tactics.**

**What's different: Weak King, fewer pieces**

Only four defenders and eight attackers. The King escapes to a corner. His weakness (sandwichable off the Throne) means every move matters — there's nowhere to hide on this board.

**Illustration: `brandub-board`**
The 7×7 starting position. All pieces shown. The small scale is the illustration — contrast it against the 11×11 grid shown earlier. No animation needed beyond the standard board pulse on corners.

---

### Ard Rí (7×7)

**The Irish "High King" variant. More pieces than Brandub, stronger King.**

**What's different: Strong King, more pieces**

Eight defenders and twelve attackers — a denser 7×7 board. The King reverts to the strong rule: he needs all four sides sealed to be captured. Corner escape. A harder fight in a tight space.

**Illustration: `ard-ri-board`**
The 7×7 Ard Rí starting position beside the Brandub one. The extra pieces are highlighted briefly (a pulse) to show the difference. Then both settle.

---

### Alea Evangelii (19×19)

**The epic. Described in an 11th-century Anglo-Saxon manuscript. The largest known Hnefatafl variant.**

**What's different: Everything is bigger**

72 attackers. 24 defenders. A 19×19 board. The same core rules apply — strong King, corner escape — but the scale transforms the game entirely. Opening theory is deeper, endgames are longer, and the King's journey to a corner is a true odyssey.

**Illustration: `alea-board`**
The full 19×19 grid shown at reduced scale — small enough to fit, detailed enough to feel massive. Attackers in their four cruciform groups near each edge. Defenders clustered in the extended cross at centre. A slow zoom-out animation reveals the full board from the centre outward.

## Illustration Style Notes

- **Line weight:** 2px strokes, rounded caps — hand-drawn feel, not rough
- **Colour:** Monochrome base with a single warm gold accent (`#c8a96e`) for highlights, trails, and flares. Background transparent.
- **Pieces:** Silhouette outlines — tall tapered shape for the King, shorter for regular pieces. Consistent with the 3D aesthetic but reduced to clean outlines.
- **Animation:** SVG + CSS keyframes only. All loops 2–4 seconds, ease-in-out. Calm and considered — never arcade.
- **Grid lines:** Thin (`1px`), slightly muted. Special squares (throne, corners) drawn with a heavier or decorated border.
- **Sequencing:** Where an illustration shows a multi-step action (capture, escape), steps animate in sequence with a pause between, then reset.

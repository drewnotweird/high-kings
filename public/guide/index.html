How to Play Hnefatafl

The core rules

Hnefatafl is asymmetric. One side plays the Attackers, who surround the board and outnumber the enemy. The other plays the Defenders, who guard a King they must escort to safety. Every variant below shares these rules:

  • The board — a square grid. The centre square is the Throne, where the King begins. The corner squares are escape squares. Only the King may stand on either.
  • Movement — every piece moves like a chess rook: any number of squares in a straight line, horizontally or vertically. No diagonals, no jumping.
  • Capture — sandwich an enemy piece between two of your own on a straight line and it is removed. Moving into a sandwich voluntarily is safe; the trap only springs when you close it.
  • Hostile squares — an empty corner (or empty Throne) counts as one half of a sandwich, so a single piece can capture against it.
  • Capturing the King — the King falls only when all four sides are sealed by attackers or hostile squares (except in "weak king" variants, where two attackers suffice once he leaves the Throne).
  • Winning — the Defenders win when the King reaches an escape square; the Attackers win when they capture him. Repeating the same position three times forfeits the game.

The eleven variants

Copenhagen

11×11 or 13×13 · the modern competitive standard

Adds the shieldwall capture: a line of defenders pressed against the board edge can be wiped out in one move by flanking both ends. Strong King, corner escape.

Play Copenhagen

Fetlar

11×11 or 13×13 · the Shetland tournament ruleset

Copenhagen's board and forces without the shieldwall — captures are always one at a time. Strong King, corner escape.

Play Fetlar

Historical

11×11 or 13×13 · reconstructed from period sources

A weak King: once he steps off the Throne, two attackers can sandwich him like any other piece. Corner escape, no shieldwall.

Play Historical

Tawlbwrdd

11×11 · the Welsh board

Edge escape — the King wins by reaching any square on the board edge, not just the corners. Shieldwall captures, strong King.

Play Tawlbwrdd

Linnaeus Tablut

9×9 · recorded by Carl Linnaeus in Lapland, 1732

The best-documented historical ruleset. Weak King, edge escape — fast and aggressive.

Play Linnaeus Tablut

Saami Tablut

9×9 · the living tradition

Linnaeus rules with a broader defensive diamond — a more open, tactical opening game.

Play Saami Tablut

Brandub

7×7 · the Irish variant

Four defenders, eight attackers, weak King, corner escape. On a board this small every move matters.

Play Brandub

Ard Rí

7×7 · the Irish High King

Eight defenders and twelve attackers packed onto the smallest board, strong King. Tight, brutal fighting.

Play Ard Rí

Tyr

15×15 · modern design by Aage Nielsen

Weak King, edge escape, and no special Throne — the centre square is ordinary. The largest competitive board.

Play Tyr

Simple Tyr

11×11 · Tyr rules on a standard board

The Tyr family's rules at a friendlier scale — a good entry point.

Play Simple Tyr

Alea Evangelii

19×19 · the epic

72 attackers, 24 defenders, one King, on a 19×19 grid recorded in a 10th-century English manuscript. The King's journey to a corner is a true odyssey.

Play Alea Evangelii

A little history

Tafl games descend from the Roman ludus latrunculorum and spread with the Vikings wherever they sailed. Game boards and pieces have been found in graves from Birka in Sweden to Dublin, and the game appears in the Norse sagas as a mark of a cultured warrior. The word hnefatafl means roughly "board of the fist-piece" — the King. Each culture the game touched left its own version: tablut in Lapland, tawlbwrdd in Wales, brandub in Ireland, alea evangelii in Anglo-Saxon England. All eleven are faithful reconstructions playable in High Kings.
